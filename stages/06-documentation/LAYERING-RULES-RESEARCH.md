# Ceramic Glaze Layering Rules - Research Compilation

*Research Date: 2026-03-16*
*Sources: DigitalFire.com, Ceramic Arts Network, potter forums (Reddit, Facebook), Glazy.org, John Britt resources*

## Confidence Levels
- **HIGH**: Well-documented, multiple sources, tested by many potters
- **MEDIUM**: Documented but fewer sources, some conflicting reports
- **LOW**: Limited documentation, anecdotal, highly variable results

---

## 1. SHINOS AS BASE (High-Soda, Low-Clay, Carbon-Trapping)

### Chemistry
- High sodium (Na2O) - creates carbon trapping potential
- High alumina (Al2O3) - usually >1.0 in unity formula
- Low clay content (20-30% in traditional recipes)
- Low silica:alumina ratio (often below 5:1)
- Low CaO (inhibits orange color)
- Soluble sodium sources (soda ash, salt) migrate to surface during drying

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: SHINOS GO FIRST (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network "The Shino Myth", Reddit r/Pottery consensus
- **When layering glazes, put the shino on FIRST**
- Using shino OVER almost any other glaze produces a disaster
- Multiple Reddit users report: "Shino on top is a flop," "real ugly results"
- **Exception**: Some commercial cone 6 shinos (Coyote) claim to work over glazes, but this is formulation-specific

#### Rule 2: SHINOS WORK WELL UNDER OTHER GLAZES (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: DigitalFire, Reddit, potter forums
- **Shinos do fine under other glazes**
- Creates carbon trap effects beneath overlying glazes
- The high-soda surface creates interesting reactions when covered

#### Rule 3: WHAT WORKS OVER SHINO (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: DigitalFire, Ceramic Arts Network, Coyote Clay testing
- **Tenmoku liners** (cone 6) - creates blushes and subtle undertones
- **Celadons** - opaque with trapped carbon and beautiful crackle
- **White/tan shinos (0-1% RIO)** under red/orange shinos (4% RIO)
- **Gloss glazes** - can create pearly effects

#### Rule 4: WHAT FAILS OVER SHINO (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network "The Shino Myth", Reddit
- **Almost ANY glaze over American shino** = disaster
- Traditional Japanese shinos may be different (very high feldspar, low clay)
- The problem: shino's high sodium and carbon trapping mechanism gets disrupted

#### Rule 5: IRON WASHES OVER SHINO (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Mike Cerv Pottery blog
- **Iron oxide washes painted on top of shino will go gunmetal and run**
- This can be intentional for effect or a problem depending on your goal

#### Rule 6: SHINO + CELADON SPECIFIC (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Source**: Reddit r/Pottery discussion
- Layer shino **under** celadon (not over)
- Result: None of the shino color, only the celadon green/blue shows
- Instead of clear, it's opaque with trapped carbon and beautiful crackle
- "This Shino is generally very nice in combinations"

#### Rule 7: APPLICATION THICKNESS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire detailed analysis
- **Thin areas**: orange/red color (from iron in clay body)
- **Thick areas**: pearly white/cream
- Color develops at glaze-body interface, showing through where glaze is thin
- Happens even on white bodies (mechanism not fully understood)

#### Rule 8: FIRING CONDITIONS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire, potter forums
- **Carbon trapping**: Start reduction early (cone 012) for best trapping
- Heavy reduction promotes carbon trapping
- Over-trapping: eliminates preferred soda-ash characteristics and color
- Traditional: wood-fired reduction
- Modern: can be emulated in gas/electric with proper schedules

### Known Successful Shino Layering Combinations
1. **Shino under Tenmoku** (cone 6) - blush effects
2. **Shino under Celadon** - carbon trap crackle over green/blue
3. **White shino (0-1% RIO) under Red/Orange shino (4% RIO)** - layered shino effects
4. **Shino under any Gloss glaze** (Coyote series) - pearly surface variations

## 2. COPPER REDS (Copper Carbonate 0.5-1.5% in Heavy Reduction)

### Chemistry
- Copper in alkaline glazes = blue (Cu2O)
- Copper in less alkaline glazes = green (CuO)
- Reduction creates red through crystallization of Cu and Cu2O
- Requires tin oxide (SnO2) as protective colloid (2-4% typical)
- Tin prevents crystals from getting too large ("livery" appearance)
- Critical: precise reduction level (CO/CO2 ratio around 1/10^5)

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: COPPER REDS ARE EXTREMELY SENSITIVE TO COMPOSITION (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire "Copper Red Glazes" by Karl Platt
- You can't simply chuck Cu-oxide into any glaze and expect red
- Requires specific chemistry to support Cu solubility
- PbO works best (but toxic) - Bismuth and Zn also help
- SnO2 essential: improves Cu solubility, controls crystal size, provides nucleation sites

#### Rule 2: COPPER RED LAYERING IS RARELY DOCUMENTED (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: Multiple searches, limited specific results
- Most copper red literature focuses on single-glaze application
- Layering risks disrupting the precise reduction balance needed
- Overlying glazes may interfere with Cu2O crystallization

#### Rule 3: COPPER RED OVER IRON-GLAZES (CONFIDENCE: LOW)
**Source**: Theoretical chemistry, limited documentation
- Risk: Iron may compete with copper for reduction
- Potential for muddy colors if both reds develop poorly
- Could be interesting if controlled properly

#### Rule 4: COPPER RED UNDER CLEAR/CELADON (CONFIDENCE: LOW)
**Source**: Potter discussion forums
- Some potters report success with very thin clear over copper red
- Risk: Disrupts crystallization mechanism
- May dull the red if overglaze is too thick

#### Rule 5: REDUCTION ATMOSPHERE IS CRITICAL (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire detailed mechanism
- Ideal reduction: equilibrium with only Cu2O and Cu-metal, NO CuO
- Too weak reduction: CuO forms with Cu2O/Cu = no red, green patches
- Too strong reduction: excess Cu metal, murky color, metallic film
- **Best**: Start reduction around 1700°F, maintain through end of firing
- Alternative: Neutral until last 2-3 hours, then reduce
- Cooling: maintain reduction or cool rapidly to 1700°F

#### Rule 6: APPLICATION THICKNESS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire, potter experience
- **Very thin films often fail** - not enough Sn remains after evaporation
- Glaze film <1mm thick means Sn evaporates readily
- Too thick = dripping, running
- **Optimal**: Medium thickness, consistent application

#### Rule 7: COOLING RATE AFFECTS COLOR (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire crystallization mechanism
- Crystals form within specific temperature range on cooling
- Too fast cooling = no color develops (can reheat to 1600-1800°F to develop)
- Hold at crystallization temperature (varies by glaze) for better reds
- Net cooling 1-3°F/minute typical in kiln cooling

#### Rule 8: REFIRING COPPER RED (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Source**: DigitalFire case study
- Possible to refire pieces that didn't develop good red
- Reheat to 1600-1800°F in reduction to develop color
- Works if original reduction was correct but cooling was too fast

### Known Copper Red Issues
- **Inconsistent results**: Change completely or cease to happen (Jane Wolters)
- **Wild variations**: Part of the appeal, frustrating for consistency
- **Requires gas/wood kiln**: Electric kilns need SiC or organic material for reduction
- **Over-reduction**: Murky, metallic, livery appearance
- **Under-reduction**: Green with red patches

## 3. CHUN BLUE (Copper + Iron Blue)

### Chemistry
- **Copper + Iron combination** creates blue (NOT cobalt)
- Traditional Chinese glaze
- Light scattering within glaze creates violet through blue to pale green range
- Reduction-fired to develop blue color

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: CHUN CONTAINS IRON, NOT COBALT (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Facebook potter group, Peter's Pottery blog
- "I'm always amazed that you see blue when this glaze contains iron oxide. No cobalt is used"
- Color comes from light scattering, not pigment
- Iron-copper interaction in reduction creates blue

#### Rule 2: CHUN OVER DIFFERENT BASES (CONFIDENCE: LOW)
**Sources**: Limited specific documentation
- Very little specific information on Chun layering
- Traditional application: single glaze
- Color depends heavily on reduction and iron interaction

#### Rule 3: CHUN LIKELY SIMILAR TO OTHER COPPER GLAZES (CONFIDENCE: LOW)
**Source**: Theoretical extrapolation from copper red knowledge
- May be sensitive to overlying glazes disrupting color mechanism
- Light scattering depends on glaze thickness and transparency
- Layering may alter the optical effects

#### Rule 4: COLOR RANGE (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Peter's Pottery blog
- Violet through blue to pale green
- Depends on light scattering within glaze
- Three glazes used in example to create color range

### Known Chun Characteristics
- Colors: violet, blue, pale green (from light scattering)
- Reduction-fired
- Contains iron oxide (no cobalt)
- Traditional Chinese glaze type
- Very limited layering documentation

## 4. TENMOKU (8-10% Iron)

### Chemistry
- **High-iron glaze** (8-10% iron oxide)
- Fired in reduction for traditional effects
- Cone 10 reduction traditional, cone 6 oxidation versions exist
- Creates glossy black to rich dark brown
- Surface effects: hare's-fur streaks, oil-spot speckles

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: GLAZES OVER TENMOKU (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Facebook groups, Pinterest, potter forums
- **Iron Lustre** over Tenmoku - breaks brown, accents nicely
- **Blue Lagoon** over Tenmoku - creates interesting variations
- **Honey Flux** over Tenmoku - adds golden highlights
- **Textured Turquoise** over Tenmoku - known combo (2x tenmoku, 2x turquoise)
- **Seaweed** over Tenmoku - part of multi-layer combo
- **Light/Dark Flux** over Tenmoku + turquoise + seaweed - "stunner" reported

#### Rule 2: GLAZES UNDER TENMOKU (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: Facebook groups, potter discussion
- **Tenmoku under Shino** - creates blushes and subtle undertones
- **Tenmoku under other glazes** - can provide dark ground for color development
- Risk: Tenmoku's high iron may affect overlying colors

#### Rule 3: TENMOKU + TURQUOISE SEQUENCE (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Facebook Mayco Mud Room Society
- **Known working combo** (application order):
  1. 2x Tenmoku
  2. 2x Textured Turquoise
  3. 2x Seaweed
  4. Light Flux all over
- Variation: Dark Flux instead of Light Flux = "stunner"
- Tested at cone 6

#### Rule 4: TENMOKU + SHINO (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network
- **Cone 6 tenmoku liners** under shino combinations
- Creates blushes to subtle undertones
- Part of Visual Arts Shino system

#### Rule 5: TENMOKU OVER/UNDER ISSUES (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Source**: Reddit glaze combination questions
- **Tenmoku + Sea Mist**: Sea Mist didn't show up much
- Possible causes: Sea Mist too thin, interaction issues
- **Variable results**: Layering effects depend heavily on specific glaze formulations

#### Rule 6: IRON CONTENT CONSIDERATIONS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: General glaze chemistry
- High iron (8-10%) will affect overlying glazes
- Can cause darkening, color shifts
- May interact with other colorants
- Best with glazes that complement or contrast with dark iron

### Known Successful Tenmoku Combinations
1. **Tenmoku + Iron Lustre** - brown with accent breaks
2. **Tenmoku + Blue Lagoon** - interesting variations
3. **Tenmoku + Honey Flux** - golden highlights
4. **2x Tenmoku + 2x Turquoise + 2x Seaweed + Flux** - layered combo
5. **Shino over Tenmoku** (cone 6) - blush effects
6. **Tenmoku as liner under Shino** - subtle undertones

## 5. CELADONS OVER DIFFERENT BASES

### Chemistry
- **Iron-based** glazes (1-3% iron typical)
- Reduction-fired for green/blue colors
- Oxidation = different colors (often amber/brown)
- Can be transparent to semi-opaque
- Traditional Chinese glaze type

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: CELADON LAYERING FOR GRADIENT EFFECT (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: Facebook Amaco groups, YouTube tutorials
- **Layering different celadons** can show underlying colors
- **Snow over Rainforest** - "beautiful" swirled pattern
- **Obsidian x2 under Rainforest x2** - dark under green
- **Overlap Snow and Rainforest** - creates pattern effects
- **Snow, Obsidian, Weeping Plum, Snow, Iron** - Amaco celadon combo system

#### Rule 2: CELADON THICKNESS GRADIENT (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Source**: Facebook Amaco Cone 5-6 Glaze Forum
- Question: Can layering create varying degrees of transparency?
- **Concern**: Top layer may conceal underlying layer
- **Mixing with clear** suggested for gradient (not layering)
- **Layering vs mixing**: Layering may not work as expected for gradients

#### Rule 3: DIFFERENT COLOR CELADON LAYERING (CONFIDENCE: LOW)
**Source**: Facebook discussion
- **Yellow over blue**: Would it be green or two distinct colors?
- **Not well documented** - results uncertain
- Likely depends on transparency and thickness

#### Rule 4: CELADON OVER VELVET UNDERGLAZE (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: YouTube tutorial "Layering Celadons over Velvet Underglazes"
- **Documented technique**: Celadons over velvet underglazes
- Creates specific effects
- Tested and reliable

#### Rule 5: AMACO CELADON + PC FLUX COMBOS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Amaco YouTube tutorials, Facebook groups
- **Celadons with PC Flux glazes** - documented combos
- **Rainforest, Obsidian, Weeping Plum, Snow, Iron** - Amaco celadon series
- **Mixing celadons** for custom colors - documented
- **Celadons over other glazes** - part of Amaco layering system

#### Rule 6: CELADON OVER SHINO (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Source**: Reddit r/Pottery
- **Shino under celadon** - opaque with trapped carbon, beautiful crackle
- **No shino color shows**, only celadon green/blue
- Instead of clear, it's opaque with carbon trap effect
- "This Shino is generally very nice in combinations"

#### Rule 7: IRON CONTENT AFFECTS COLOR (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network "Techno File: Iron in Celadons"
- Iron-colored glazes: celadon, chün, temmoku, kaki, teadust, shino
- **Iron amount and type** affects color development
- **Reduction critical** for celadon colors
- Different iron oxides (RIO, black iron) create different effects

### Known Successful Celadon Combinations
1. **Snow over Rainforest** - swirled patterns
2. **Obsidian under Rainforest** - dark under green
3. **Overlap Snow and Rainforest** - pattern effects
4. **Celadons over Velvet underglazes** - defined underglaze with celadon surface
5. **Shino under Celadon** - carbon trap crackle over celadon
6. **Amaco celadon layering system** - multiple celadon combinations

## 6. RUTILE GLAZES LAYERED WITH OTHER COLORANTS

### Chemistry
- **Rutile (TiO2)** - titanium dioxide, natural mineral
- Contains impurities: niobium, rare earth metals
- Creates opalescence, subtle color, and texture
- **Fickle**: inconsistent between sources
- **Not a colorant itself** - enhances/reacts with other colorants
- Can create blue, gold, brown, pink depending on base and firing

### Layering Rules

#### Rule 1: RUTILE + IRON (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Glazy, Facebook groups, YouTube tutorials
- **3 Iron Luster + 3 Blue Rutile + 1 Honey Flux** - documented combo
- Creates complex breaking and variation
- **Rutile over iron** - can create gold/brown tones
- **Iron under rutile** - enhances rutile's reactive effects

#### Rule 2: RUTILE + COBALT (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: YouTube tutorial, glazeshare.com
- **Snow over Blue Rutile over Cobalt** - layered combo documented
- Creates layered color effects
- Rutile between white and cobalt creates intermediate tones

#### Rule 3: RUTILE + COPPER (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: General glaze chemistry, limited specific combo docs
- Rutile can enhance copper blues and greens
- May create turquoise or teal tones
- **Less documented** than iron/cobalt combinations

#### Rule 4: BLUE RUTILE SPECIFIC COMBOS (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Pinterest, Facebook groups, YouTube
- **Oatmeal over Blue Rutile** - popular combo
- **Smokey Merlot + Blue Rutile** - variations documented
- **Blue Rutile + Iron Lustre + Oatmeal** - layering combo
- **Blue Rutile under various glazes** - breaks brown, creates accents

#### Rule 5: RUTILE AS INTERMEDIATE LAYER (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: Layering discussions, test results
- Rutile works well between contrasting glazes
- Creates color transitions and variations
- **Example**: Cobalt → Rutile → Snow (white)

#### Rule 6: RUTILE IS FINICKY (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network "Rutile" recipe
- "Rutile is just as fickle as it is beautiful"
- **Composition varies between mined sources**
- Rare metals (niobium) create rainbow colors, opalescence
- Inconsistent performance - testing essential

#### Rule 7: RUTILE CRYSTALLIZATION (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: Ceramic Arts Network, Glazy
- Creates **micro-crystalline** effects
- Surface texture: crystalline
- Opalescence from suspended crystals
- Firing temperature affects crystal size and effect

#### Rule 8: BLUE RUTILE APPLICATION (CONFIDENCE: MEDIUM)
**Sources**: Amaco YouTube tutorials
- **Amaco PC-20 Blue Rutile** variations documented
- Application thickness affects color development
- Works over/under various Amaco glazes
- Part of Amaco Potter's Choice layering system

### Known Successful Rutile Combinations
1. **3 Iron Luster + 3 Blue Rutile + 1 Honey Flux** - complex breaking
2. **Snow over Blue Rutile over Cobalt** - layered tones
3. **Oatmeal over Blue Rutile** - popular combo
4. **Smokey Merlot + Blue Rutile** - color variations

## GENERAL LAYERING PRINCIPLES

### Application Order (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: DigitalFire, Ceramic Arts Network, potter consensus
1. **First layer**: Glaze with higher thermal expansion (usually darker/more fluid)
2. **Second layer**: Glaze with lower thermal expansion (usually lighter/stiffer)
3. **General rule**: Dark/stiff → Light/fluid (for bubble revelation effects)
4. **Exception**: Shino always goes first (under) when layering

### Thermal Expansion Considerations (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire glaze chemistry
- **Crazing risk**: High expansion glaze over low expansion glaze
- **Shino issue**: Very high thermal expansion (crazing common)
- **Solution**: Trade sodium for lithia, zinc, or boron to lower expansion
- **Testing**: Essential for layered combinations

### Glaze Fluidity Interaction (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Source**: DigitalFire "Glaze Layering"
- **Stiffer matte over fluid glossy** = variegation
- **Bubbles reveal underlying color** where glaze is more fluid
- **Calcium carbonate** encourages bubbling for this effect
- **Example**: White matte over dark glossy at cone 6

### Firing Atmosphere Effects (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Multiple sources
- **Reduction glazes over oxidation glazes**: May not develop properly
- **Oxidation over reduction**: Can disrupt reduction effects
- **Consistent atmosphere** usually best for predictable results
- **Multi-atmosphere firings**: Advanced technique, variable results

### Testing Recommendations (CONFIDENCE: HIGH)
**Sources**: Glazy.org, DigitalFire, potter experience
1. **Test tiles first** - never commit to a full piece without testing
2. **Triaxial blends** - systematic testing of ratios
3. **Different clays** - results vary by clay body
4. **Different thicknesses** - application thickness affects results
5. **Document everything** - glaze recipes, application, firing schedule
6. **Fire in different kiln locations** - temperature variation affects results
