# Material Safety

## Silica Dust Hazards

### Overview
Crystalline silica is a common mineral found in clay, sand, stone, and ceramic materials. When processed, it creates **respirable particles 100× smaller than ordinary sand** that can cause serious lung diseases.

### Health Effects
- **Silicosis** - incurable lung disease leading to disability and death
- **Lung cancer** - classified as human lung carcinogen
- **Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)**
- **Kidney disease**
- **Symptoms:** cough, shortness of breath, chest pain, weakness, wheezing

### Exposure Limits

| Limit Type | Limit | Time Period | Source |
|------------|-------|-------------|--------|
| **OSHA PEL** | 50 μg/m³ | 8-hour TWA | 29 CFR 1910.1053 |
| **NIOSH REL** | 50 μg/m³ | 10-hour TWA, 40-hour week | Considers quartz carcinogen |
| **ACGIH TLV** | 0.05 mg/m³ | - | For respirable quartz |

**Note:** μg/m³ = micrograms per cubic meter; mg/m³ = milligrams per cubic meter

### Protection Measures

#### Engineering Controls
- Local exhaust ventilation at dust generation points
- Enclosed automatic material handling systems
- **HEPA vacuum cleaners** - NEVER dry sweep
- Wet methods to suppress dust
- Proper kiln ventilation (minimum 100-150 fpm face velocity)
- Eliminate fans that re-entrain settled dust

#### Work Practices
- Wet-wipe surfaces instead of sweeping or vacuuming
- Use squeegee-type brooms for cleaning
- Clean green castings rather than white castings when possible
- Mist white pieces with water before cleaning
- Assign dedicated cleaning staff
- Use non-crystalline silica mold release (perlite)

#### Respiratory Protection
| Protection Level | Type | Requirements |
|-----------------|------|--------------|
| **Minimum** | N95 NIOSH-approved respirator | Fit testing required |
| **Higher exposure** | P100 or R95 | For oil aerosols present |
| **Requirements** | - | Fit testing, proper storage, clean-shaven for seal |

#### Housekeeping
- No carpet in work areas
- Use HEPA vacuum only (adequate capacity required)
- Wet mopping preferred
- Regular cleaning schedule

### High Exposure Activities
- Mixing dry clay and glazes
- Bag dumping and material transfer
- Spray glazing
- Sanding and grinding greenware
- Slip house operations
- Cast shop cleaning and finishing
- Glaze preparation and mixing

## Common Material Safety Notes

### Barium (Barium Carbonate - BaCO₃)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | Poisonous if ingested. Causes violent diarrhea, convulsive tremors, muscular paralysis. Affects heart and nervous system. |
| **Exposure Limits** | ACGIH TLV: 0.5 mg/m³, OSHA PEL: 0.5 mg/m³ |
| **Food Safety** | EPA drinking water standard: 2.0 mg/L (2 ppm). Safe leach level: 1 mg/L. Low risk in fired ware (converts to insoluble barium sulfate). |
| **Common Use** | 0.1-0.6% in clay bodies to precipitate soluble salts |
| **Precautions** | Do not use in food-contact glazes. Wear respirator when mixing dry. Use gloves. Never ingest raw material. |

### Lead (Lead Compounds)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | **EXTREMELY TOXIC** - cumulative poison affecting nervous system, especially in children |
| **Health Effects** | Learning disabilities, developmental delays, lower IQ, neurological damage, reproductive problems |
| **Food Safety** | FDA has specific extraction limits. Lead-glazed pottery must be properly fired to bind lead. |
| **Problem Types** | Handmade with crude appearance, antique/damaged ware, brightly decorated (orange/red/yellow), from flea markets/street vendors |
| **Testing** | Lead test kits available at hardware stores - swab changes color if lead present |
| **Warning** | **No amount of washing, boiling, or other process can remove lead from pottery** |
| **Recommendation** | **NEVER use lead in functional ware.** Use only for decorative items marked "Not for Food Use" |

### Cadmium (Cadmium Compounds)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | Known human carcinogen, toxic if ingested or inhaled |
| **Health Effects** | Kidney damage, bone demineralization, cancer risk, respiratory irritation |
| **Uses** | Red, orange, yellow colorants |
| **Fuming** | Can fume at firing temperatures - requires kiln ventilation |
| **Food Safety** | FDA has strict leaching limits for ceramicware |
| **Precautions** | Respirator required for dry powders. Gloves required. Kiln ventilation mandatory. Consider alternatives for functional ware. |

### Chromium

| Form | Toxicity |
|------|----------|
| **Chromium III** | Relatively safe, essential nutrient |
| **Chromium VI** | **Known human carcinogen**, highly toxic |

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Hazards** | Lung cancer (Cr-VI), skin ulcers and irritation, kidney/liver damage, respiratory problems |
| **Ceramic Use** | Green colorants, glaze chemistry |
| **Fuming** | Can fume during firing - kiln ventilation required |
| **Precautions** | Know your chromium form. Respirator for dry handling. Avoid Cr-VI if possible. Proper ventilation essential. |

### Nickel

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | Probable human carcinogen |
| **Health Effects** | Skin sensitization/allergic reactions, respiratory irritation, cancer risk with long-term exposure |
| **Ceramic Use** | Colorants |
| **Precautions** | Avoid skin contact. Respirator for dry powders. Gloves required. |

### Lithium (Lithium Carbonate)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | Toxic if ingested, affects nervous system |
| **Health Effects** | Nausea, vomiting, neurological effects, thyroid effects |
| **Ceramic Use** | Flux in glazes |
| **Precautions** | Do not ingest. Avoid breathing dust. Gloves recommended. |

### Manganese (Manganese Dioxide)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Toxicity** | **Neurotoxin** - linked to Parkinson's-like symptoms |
| **Health Effects** | Manganism (Parkinson's-like disease), neurological damage, respiratory problems |
| **Ceramic Use** | Purple/brown colorants, glaze additives |
| **Fuming Danger** | **HIGH** - can fume at certain temperatures, stays airborne |
| **Exposure Limits** | Specific OSHA PEL exists due to neurotoxicity |
| **Precautions** | **Kiln ventilation MANDATORY.** Respirator for dry handling. Avoid high percentages. Consider alternatives for functional ware. |

### Generally Safe Colorants

| Material | Notes |
|----------|-------|
| Iron oxide (red, yellow, black, brown) | Safe |
| Cobalt | Safe in small amounts (toxic but used in low percentages) |
| Copper | Generally safe in fired ware |
| Rutile (titanium dioxide) | Safe |
| Zirconium | Safe encapsulating agent |
| Tin | Safe opacifier |
| Zinc | Safe in glazes |

**Important:** Even "safe" materials should be handled with respiratory protection in dry powder form.

## Studio Safety Guidelines

### PPE Requirements

#### Respirators

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **When Required** | Mixing dry clay/glazes, sanding/grinding/drilling, spray glazing, cleaning dusty surfaces, any dry powders, dusty kiln loading/unloading |
| **Minimum Rating** | N95 NIOSH-approved |
| **Recommended** | P100 for highest protection |
| **Fit Requirements** | Must be fit-tested, clean-shaven for seal, straps positioned, check seal before each use |
| **Maintenance** | Store in clean/dry place, replace filters regularly, inspect for damage, follow manufacturer guidelines |

#### Gloves

| Type | Use |
|------|-----|
| **Nitrile** | General chemical protection |
| **Rubber** | Heavy metal handling |
| **Glove Liners** | Comfort during extended use |

**When Required:** Mixing glazes with heavy metals, handling toxic materials (barium, lead, cadmium), cleaning kiln shelves with chemicals, any skin-irritating materials

#### Eye Protection

**When Required:** Mixing glazes, grinding/sanding, using wire tools, kiln maintenance, any flying particle risk

**Types:** Safety glasses, goggles for liquid splashes

### Ventilation Standards

#### General Requirements
- Adequate outdoor air intake
- Proper air changes per hour
- Consult ventilation engineer for specific parameters

#### Local Exhaust Ventilation

| Location Required | Performance |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Mixing/pugging areas | Minimum 100-150 fpm face velocity |
| Glaze spray booths | Capture dust as close to generation as possible |
| Sanding/grinding stations | Minimize bends in ductwork for efficiency |
| Kiln areas | - |

#### Kiln Ventilation

| Purpose | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Remove** | Carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, nitrogen dioxide, fluoride compounds, metal fumes (manganese, cadmium, lead) |
| **Requirements** | Vent to outside, never vent into occupied space, consider dedicated system (especially gas kilns) |

**Emissions:**
- Carbon monoxide (odorless, deadly)
- Carbon dioxide
- Sulfur dioxide (up to 1000°C)
- Nitrogen dioxide
- Fluoride compounds
- Metal fumes

### Safe Cleanup Practices

#### Dry Methods - PROHIBITED
- NEVER sweep with broom
- NEVER use ordinary vacuum
- NEVER use compressed air to blow dust

#### Recommended Methods
- Wet-wipe all surfaces
- Use HEPA vacuum with adequate capacity
- Wet mop floors
- Use squeegee brooms for initial cleanup
- Mist dusty areas before cleanup

#### General Hygiene
- Don't eat, drink, or store food in work areas
- Wash hands thoroughly before eating
- Wet methods for dust control
- Clean work area daily
- Avoid carpets - they trap dust

### Ergonomics

#### Workstation Heights
| Requirement | Measurement |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Minimum** | 27.6 inches |
| **Maximum** | 56.2 inches |
| **Purpose** | Eliminate overhead reaching and bending |

#### Material Handling
- Store frequently used materials at waist height
- Use scissor lift tables to reduce bending
- Eliminate lifting items over 50 pounds
- Use carts for heavy materials
- Provide faucet hose extension to eliminate lifting buckets

#### Repetitive Motion
- Don't perform repetitive activities in long sessions
- Vary tasks throughout day
- Take regular breaks
- Use adjustable stools with lumbar support
- Personalize wheel and stool heights for each user

## Food Safety Testing

### Functional Ware Considerations

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Leaching** | Glazes can leach heavy metals into food and drink through acid attack |
| **Bacteria Harboring** | Crazed glazes can harbor bacteria in cracks |
| **Flaking** | Poorly adhered glazes can flake off into food |

### Testing Methods

#### Home Testing
| Method | What It Tests | Limitations |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| **Lead test kits** | Lead presence | Swab changes color if lead present. Only tests for lead, not other metals |

**Availability:** Hardware stores

#### Laboratory Testing

| Types | - |
|-------|---|
| **Methods** | Atomic absorption spectroscopy, ICP-MS (Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry) |
| **Test Labs** | Elemental Research Lab (North Vancouver, BC), Kirby Health Center Lab (Wilkes-Barre, PA), commercial testing laboratories |
| **What to Test For** | Lead, cadmium, barium, other heavy metals in your glazes |

### FDA Standards

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Regulation** | FDA Compliance Policy Guides (CPGs) set limits on leachable lead and cadmium |
| **Lead Guidance** | Specific extraction limits by category (flatware, hollowware, etc.) |
| **Traditional Pottery** | Special concerns for improperly fired traditional pottery |
| **Labeling** | Ornamental ware must be marked "Not for Food Use - May Poison Food" |

### Best Practices

#### Glaze Selection
- Use only food-safe tested commercial glazes for functional ware
- Test custom glazes before using for food
- Avoid toxic colorants in food-contact glazes
- Properly fire to maturity - underfired glazes leach more

#### Design Considerations
- Avoid crazing (use proper fit)
- Ensure full coverage
- Proper foot finishing
- Test with acid foods if intended for acidic contents

#### Documentation
- Keep records of glaze recipes
- Document firing schedules
- Maintain test results
- Label clearly as food-safe or decorative-only

## SDS (Safety Data Sheets)

### Purpose
SDS provide detailed information on physical and chemical properties, health hazards, and safe handling procedures.

### Where to Find SDS

#### Suppliers
- All ceramic material suppliers must provide SDS
- Available on supplier websites
- Request by email or phone
- Often provided with purchase

#### Online Resources
- Euclids SDS collection
- Sheffield Pottery SDS database
- Clay Art Center SDS
- Mayco Colors SDS
- Clay Planet SDS sheets

### Key Sections to Review

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **Section 1** | Identification - Product name, manufacturer, emergency contact |
| **Section 2** | Hazard Identification - Health effects, warning statements |
| **Section 3** | Composition/Ingredients - What's in the product |
| **Section 4** | First Aid Measures - What to do if exposed |
| **Section 5** | Fire-Fighting Measures |
| **Section 6** | Accidental Release Measures - Spill cleanup |
| **Section 7** | Handling and Storage |
| **Section 8** | Exposure Controls/PPE - What protection to use |
| **Section 9** | Physical and Chemical Properties |
| **Section 11** | Toxicological Information - Health effects data |
| **Section 12** | Ecological Information |

### Understanding Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Meaning |
|--------------|---------|
| **TLV** | Threshold Limit Value (ACGIH recommended exposure limit) |
| **PEL** | Permissible Exposure Limit (OSHA legal limit) |
| **REL** | Recommended Exposure Limit (NIOSH recommendation) |
| **LD50** | Lethal Dose 50% - dose that kills half of test subjects |
| **mg/m³** | Milligrams per cubic meter of air |
| **μg/m³** | Micrograms per cubic meter of air |
| **ppm** | Parts per million |
| **TWA** | Time-Weighted Average - average over specified time period |

## Actionable Summary

### Immediate Actions
- Get SDS for all materials in your studio
- Assess current ventilation and dust control
- Purchase appropriate respirator(s) and get fit-tested
- Replace any lead-containing glazes with safe alternatives
- Implement wet cleanup methods - stop dry sweeping
- Add kiln ventilation if not present

### Priority Materials to Avoid
- Lead in any functional ware
- Cadmium in food-contact glazes
- High-manganese glazes without proper ventilation
- Barium carbonate in food-contact surfaces
- Chromium VI compounds

### Essential Equipment
- NIOSH-approved respirator (N95 minimum, P100 recommended)
- HEPA vacuum cleaner
- Local exhaust ventilation at dust sources
- Kiln vent system
- Gloves for chemical handling
- Safety glasses or goggles
- Wet mop and squeegee

### Training Priorities
- Respirator fit testing and proper use
- Safe material handling procedures
- Spill cleanup procedures
- Kiln safety and emergency procedures
- Recognition of hazardous materials

### Regular Practices
- Daily wet cleanup of work areas
- Regular inspection of ventilation systems
- Replace respirator filters on schedule
- Review SDS when introducing new materials
- Test functional ware for food safety
- Monitor for dust accumulation
