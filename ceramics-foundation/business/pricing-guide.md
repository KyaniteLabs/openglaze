# Pricing Guide for Pottery Sales

**Purpose:** Cost-based pricing formulas and business practices for pottery sales
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-15
**Sources:** ClayShare Resources, Ceramic Arts Network, CraftCalc, Old Forge Creations

---

## Cost-Based Pricing Formula

### Standard Ceramics Pricing Formula

```
Materials + Labor + Overhead = Cost
Cost × 2 = Wholesale Price
Wholesale × 2 = Retail Price
```

**Effective Markup:** Retail is typically 4× Cost (2× wholesale × 2× retail)

---

### Detailed Formula Components

**Full Formula:**
```
(Clay + Glazes + Firing + Decorations) + Labor + Overhead × Markup = Retail Price
```

#### Materials

- Clay
- Glazes
- Slip
- Underglaze
- Lusters
- Firing costs
- Decorations

#### Labor

**Hourly Rate Range:** $25-$75 per hour for skilled workers

**Note:** US Department of Labor lists artists' average hourly wage as $24.58

**Calculation:** Hourly Rate × Hours Spent = Labor Cost

#### Overhead

**Percentage Method:** 10% of (Materials + Labor) combined costs

**Items to Include:**
- Studio Rent & Utilities
- Gallery Fees & Show/Booth Expenses
- Books, Videos, Workshops, Residencies
- Conferences & Travel Expenses
- Etsy/PayPal fees
- Packing & Shipping Fees
- Memberships, Co-ops, Guilds & Organization Fees
- Website & Advertising (Business Cards, Mailings)
- Pottery (Research and Development)
- Studio Equipment (see equipment calculation)
- Tools, Studio and Office Supplies (Computer/Software)

#### Equipment Depreciation

**Formula:**
```
Equipment Cost ÷ Years of Use = Annual Cost
Annual Cost ÷ 12 = Monthly Cost
Monthly Cost ÷ Monthly Production = Per-Piece Equipment Cost
```

**Example:**
```
$5000 kiln ÷ 10 years = $500/year
$500 ÷ 12 months = $41.67/month
$41.67 ÷ 41 pots/month = $1.02 per pot
```

## Worked Examples

### Highly Decorated Mug (Pricing Constraint Example)

| Component | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| Materials | $4.00 |
| Labor | 3 hours × $30/hour = $90.00 |
| Overhead | 10% of ($4 + $90) = $9.40 |
| **Total Cost** | **$103.40** |
| Wholesale Price | $103.40 × 2 = $206.80 |
| Retail Price | $206.80 × 2 = $413.60 |

**Constraint Note:** Can you realistically sell a mug for $413? Maybe but highly unlikely. This illustrates pricing constraints - you may need to adjust production methods or target different markets.

---

### Standard Mug (Realistic Example)

| Component | Cost |
|-----------|------|
| Materials | $10.00 |
| Labor | 30 minutes × $15/hour = $7.50 |
| Overhead | 10% of ($10 + $7.50) = $1.75 |
| **Total Cost** | **$19.25** |
| Wholesale Price | $19.25 × 2 = $38.50 |
| Retail Price | $38.50 × 2 = $77.00 |

## Wholesale vs. Retail Pricing

### Standard Relationship

| Formula | Calculation |
|---------|-------------|
| Wholesale | Cost × 2 = Wholesale Price |
| Retail | Wholesale × 2 = Retail Price |
| Effective Markup | Retail is typically 4× Cost |

**Definition:** Selling wholesale means selling multiples to a single buyer or store owner at a lower price while still being profitable due to volume.

### Minimum Order Quantities

- **Typical Range:** $300-$500 minimum orders
- **Purpose:** Protects profit margins by ensuring order is worth time and materials invested

### Wholesale Considerations

| Consideration | Guidance |
|---------------|----------|
| **Profit Warning** | Labor is NOT profit - you must build actual profit into prices to allow for future expansion (hiring apprentices, etc.) |
| **Volume Benefit** | Wholesale can be cheaper than retail due to volume efficiencies, but must still include profit margin |
| **Pricing Consistency** | Keep wholesale and retail pricing consistent to maintain gallery relationships |

## Gallery Commission Structures

### Standard Commissions

| Artwork Type | Typical Commission |
|--------------|-------------------|
| 2D Artwork (paintings, photos, monotypes) | 50% |
| 3D Artwork (ceramics, sculpture) | 33.3% - 40% |
| General Range | 30-50% is fair |

**Note:** Galleries use markup to cover marketing and exhibition costs.

### Consignment vs. Wholesale

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Consignment** | Work remains yours until sold, gallery takes commission percentage |
| **Wholesale** | Gallery purchases work outright at wholesale price, then marks up for retail |
| **Pricing Consistency** | Set equivalent prices for studio and galleries to maintain positive relationships |

### Pricing for Galleries

| Rule | Guidance |
|------|----------|
| **Planning** | Plan to sell pieces at gallery retail price (including markup) rather than undercutting |
| **Warning** | Undercutting gallery pricing on your own site or studio sale could result in galleries being less inclined to work with you |
| **Uniform Pricing** | Be uniform with pricing - galleries typically mark up 50% or more |

## Market-Based Pricing Factors

### Regional Differences

**Principle:** Where you sell work greatly affects what you can sell it for.

**Example:** Selling at farmer's market has different price points than selling in a gallery.

### Experience Level

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Experience | Someone making pots for 10 years can and should sell work for more than someone making pots for 6 months |
| Skills | Longer experience = better skills + following = higher prices |
| Progression | Start lower and raise prices over time as you build reputation and skill |

### Uniqueness Premium

| Piece Type | Pricing |
|------------|---------|
| One-of-a-kind | Should sell for more than production work |
| Production work | Same simple undecorated form made repeatedly = lower price point |

### Market Research Methods

1. Research similar artists' prices on Etsy, Instagram, independent websites
2. Look at artists with similar skill level and time investment
3. Attend auctions to let bidding determine market value
4. Compare to gallery prices for similar work

**Caution:** Artists you compare with may be more experienced, so their prices might be higher - adjust accordingly.

## Common Pricing Mistakes

### Underpricing Pitfalls

| Mistake | Reality |
|---------|----------|
| Underselling pieces or not paying living wage | Producing artwork is expensive - raw materials, tools, equipment, education, studio overhead, insurance, hours invested |
| Guidance | Consider reasonable hourly wage (USDOL average: $24.58/hour for artists) and work backward |

### Not Accounting for All Costs

**Forgotten Costs:**
- Equipment depreciation
- Research and development
- Business management time
- Shipping/packaging
- Marketing/advertising
- Professional development (workshops, conferences)

**Common Error:** Many forget to add overhead percentage.

### Emotional Pricing

| Theory | Reality |
|--------|----------|
| Some pots are 'on scholarship' from others | Not practical - each piece needs to cover its own costs |
| Lose money on one piece, make it up on another | Never be assured a customer will return or buy a more expensive piece |

### Labor Confusion

| Common Error | Correction |
|--------------|------------|
| Thinking labor cost IS profit | Labor is for your production time NOW - profit is for future expansion (hiring, growth) |
| Consequence | Without building in profit, you can never expand beyond solo production |

### Pricing Constraint Ignorance

| Mistake | Reality | Solution |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Pricing without considering what market will bear | May calculate a 'correct' price of $400 for a mug that no one will pay | Consider production efficiency, target market, and whether to make the piece at all |

### Inconsistent Pricing

| Mistake | Consequence | Solution |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| Charging different prices in different venues | Customers who buy at location A then see lower price at location B will be unhappy | Always price work the same regardless of selling location (exception: sales/promotions) |

### Starting Too High

| Guidance | Reason | Exception |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| Always start lower and go up | Very difficult to bring prices down and explain to previous customers why they paid more | Sales or special discounts are acceptable |

## Additional Pricing Considerations

### Tiered Pricing Strategy

| Concept | Benefit |
|---------|---------|
| Have various price points to reach wider audience | Less expensive pieces are more obtainable for young collectors or modest budgets |
| More people buying = more exposure = word-of-mouth marketing | Customers can start with lower-priced pieces and upgrade to higher-priced artwork when able |

### Profit Margin Targets

| Target | Metric |
|--------|--------|
| Operating margin target | 15-20% operating margins |
| COGS optimization | Target COGS reduction from 12% to 9% |
| Material margin | If material margin percentage is greater than 40%, typically have sufficient margin |

### Documentation

| Recommendation | Purpose |
|----------------|---------|
| Use a spreadsheet to track materials and overhead | Thoroughly record working hours for accurate labor calculation |
| Spreadsheet will tell you | Material/firing costs per piece, monthly production capacity, and recommended pricing |

## Key Takeaways

1. Use consistent formula: Materials + Labor + Overhead × 2 (wholesale) × 2 (retail)
2. Labor rate: $25-$75/hour for skilled work (USDOL average: $24.58)
3. Overhead: 10% of materials + labor combined
4. Don't forget equipment depreciation calculation
5. Gallery commissions: 33-50% typical
6. Wholesale = 50% of retail with minimum order quantities
7. Each piece must cover its own costs - no 'scholarship' pieces
8. Start pricing lower, raise over time - never cut prices
9. Maintain consistent pricing across all sales channels
10. Build profit into prices, not just labor recovery
11. Research comparable artists but adjust for experience level
12. Consider what market will bear, not just what you need to charge
13. Track everything in spreadsheets for accurate calculations
